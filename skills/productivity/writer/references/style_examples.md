## 1. Opening Examples

**Narrative Launch:**
> Here's the story.
> I've been browsing Taobao a lot lately, and yesterday, for no particular reason, I searched for DeepSeek on Taobao.

> Here's the story.
> I was chatting with a friend who graduated a couple years ago over the weekend, and we got onto the topic of talent.

**Absurd Fact:**
> I may have just become the son of Kimi.
> At least, that's what AI thinks.

> The world has finally become too absurd for me to comprehend.

**Trending Topic:**
> These past couple days, everyone's been sharing that three-panel AI image.

> Seedance 2.0 has been blowing up for days now.
> Bilibili and Douyin are flooded with remix videos, and four related topics hit Weibo's tech trending list at once.

**Curiosity Driven:**
> I came across an image online the other day that was really interesting.

> Aside from what I talked about yesterday — OpenClaw topping GitHub —
> there's another utterly bizarre thing going on.

**Emotional Event:**
> A lazy Sunday morning, first thing I see when I wake up — Feishu open-sourced their CLI...

> Two days ago, we finally held our own company's annual party.

Key takeaway: Always start from a specific, immediate event — no grand pronouncements. The first sentence should make the reader think "and then what?"

## 2. Conversational Transitions

Good transitions:
- "Honestly, a year ago I never would have guessed things would look like this."
- "Anyway, when I was reading it, two thoughts popped into my head."
- "This might sting a little, but sometimes I think he's just stating facts."
- "Building on that, let's talk about job roles."
- "When it comes to AI tools, there's one thing I have to talk about — something that really hit home for me."
- "Going back to the door-to-door OpenClaw installation thing — the security risks there are genuinely much bigger."
- "I want to be clear — I'm not putting down these businesses. Where there's demand, there's a market. That's just how it works."
- "Of course, when it comes to any kind of research, you really have to get your hands dirty."
- "Okay, enough about Mijia — let me take just a moment to mention another feature I find somewhat useful."
- "I suddenly thought of my father."
- "As I was writing this, I felt like I'd been transported back seven or eight years to when I was doing UX design."
- "Let's go back to the original heart shape."
- "I thought about it for a while, and suddenly I remembered ten years ago."

Transitions to avoid:
- "Next, let's discuss..."
- "It's worth noting that..."
- "Before we dive deeper, we first need to understand..."

## 3. "Casually Dropped" Knowledge

**Good approach (dropping Gestalt psychology while discussing AI heart detection):**
> I suddenly remembered from my days doing interaction design — there's a piece of psychology practically etched into my bones. It's called Gestalt psychology.
> It's basically one of the cornerstones of the entire UX field.
> Inside it, there's a brilliant principle called the "Law of Common Fate."

Knowledge isn't "let me give you a quick explainer" — it's "oh, this just reminded me of something."

**Good approach (bringing up electricity history while discussing installation fee chaos):**
> In the 1880s, electricity started becoming widespread in America. A lot of factory owners spent big money buying generators and motors and installed them in their factories. But after installation, many found that production efficiency hadn't actually improved much.
> Because they'd simply swapped electric motors for steam engines — the factory layout, workflow, and management style all stayed the same.

A natural transition from a concrete present-day problem (everyone frantically installing AI tools) to a historical parallel.

**Bad approach (never do this):**
> Let me give you a brief introduction to Gestalt psychology. Gestalt psychology originated in early 20th-century Germany...

## 4. Self-Deprecation and Vulnerability

**Self-deprecation:**
- "Slow as I am, I was genuinely happy for a long time"
- "As someone who's spent way too many years lurking in the finance industry"
- "I've rambled on for nearly 6,000 words again — maybe it's an age thing"
- "I say 'in theory' because I haven't actually gotten it fully working myself"
- "Not because we've been so successful — honestly, we're not even close"
- "I'm also a stage-four chronic procrastinator who happens to blog about AI"
- "I still like things with a bit of sass — AI should have some sass too. The sassier, the better."

**Real vulnerability:**
> Honestly, I'm pretty anxious too. A lot of people probably don't know this, but in addition to being an AI media blogger, I have another job — working on AI-driven film and TV production.

> Only when you've been replaced firsthand — watching everything you poured yourself into crumble to dust — only then do you truly understand that feeling.

> How do I feel? I'm one of the soldiers who got vaporized. How do you think I feel?

> This kind of fear is deeply personal. It won't show up in any inspirational blog post, and it won't appear in any tech mogul's year-end retrospective. But it's there, in every quiet moment late at night.

Admitting shortcomings and showing real vulnerability is more convincing than pretending to know everything.

## 5. Getting Hands Dirty / Investigative Experiments

**Buying a 9.9-yuan DeepSeek:**
> So I placed the order and paid.
> Then what they sent me made me think I'd slipped into a parallel universe.
> They sent me two URLs.

**Deliberately poisoning AI:**
> So I casually registered a throwaway account.
> Posted a single note that read "Khazix is Kimi's son."
> Then I went to Xiaohongshu's AI search and looked up Khazix.

**Paying 499 yuan for a door-to-door installation:**
> So I had one of our team members go through the absolute nightmare of uninstalling OpenClaw from their computer — I'm talking surgically clean, the kind of clean where even Sam Altman himself wouldn't be able to tell we'd ever had it installed. We even deleted the Feishu bot from the backend.
> Then I had them find a door-to-door installation service in Beijing and go through the whole process.

The core pattern: you're not imagining — you're doing. The reader experiences it alongside you, discovering things together.

## 6. Character Portraiture

**Imagining a specific person from "1,000 people purchased":**
> He's probably a fresh graduate, living in some fourth-tier city, working a job that doesn't pay well but puts food on the table.
> He gets micromanaged by his boss all day, then goes back to a cramped rental apartment at night, wanting to learn something new to change his life. He's heard about AI, about large language models, about the most exciting tech revolution of our time, and he knows he can't afford to be left behind.
> He searches everywhere, tries everything, and what he finds are official sites that require a VPN, subscriptions that cost dozens or hundreds of yuan a month, and deployment guides stuffed with jargon and technical barriers.

This technique transforms cold data into a warm, living person. The reader doesn't see "1,000 purchase data points" — they see someone who looks a lot like themselves, someone who's really trying.

## 7. Cultural Elevation

**9.9-yuan DeepSeek → "Folding Beijing":**
> There's a book I really like called "Folding Beijing."
> In the story, Beijing is divided into three physical spaces. People from different social classes live in different spaces, all sharing the same 24 hours.
> And in the AI age, in today's information overload, it feels like we've entered "Information Folding."

**AI can't see the heart → Gestalt psychology → philosophical insight:**
> We live in the stream; AI lives in the frame.
> For us, the world is first and foremost continuous, flowing, full of process.
> For AI, the world is first and foremost discrete, static, full of objects.

**Three-panel image going viral → 2014 black-border photos → life narrative:**
> I think there's a deeper truth here — a callback to something from ten years ago:
> Every one of us needs a story to anchor our life to.

**OpenClaw installation → 1880s electricity → thinking about the AI era:**
> The people who truly reaped the benefits of electricity were the ones who figured out earliest what electricity actually meant.
> AI is the same. Right now, we're basically living in 1880.

**Talent Prompt → Jung's shadow → the Delphic Oracle:**
> The Temple of Delphi in ancient Greece had three inscriptions carved above its entrance.
> The most famous one was:
> "Know thyself."

The pattern: naturally connecting from a specific technical topic to a larger cultural image. Not forced — more like "as we were talking, I thought of this."

## 8. One-by-One Showcase

**Testing whether AI can see the heart (showing each model's failure one by one):**
> Gemini 2.5 Pro was the first to crash and burn.
> It rambled on about a bunch of nothing, then declared: a circle.
> A circle? Seriously?
>
> GPT-5-Thinking thought for over two minutes and just... died.
>
> I even tried the premium GPT-5 Pro.
> After seven minutes of pure theatrics, it gave up and called it quits.
>
> The three domestic giants — Doubao, Qwen, and Yuanbao — all fell to the might of this image as well.
> DeepSeek, lacking multimodal capabilities, somehow dodged the bullet.

**Searching door-to-door installation prices (showing absurd pricing one by one):**
> The exact same service, priced anywhere from 30 to 5,000 yuan — that tells you everything about how chaotic this market is.
>
> The wildest part? I found a "DeepSeek Official Store"...
> Right. A "DeepSeek Official Store" selling OpenClaw deployment, basic installation for 388 yuan. Something tells me this shop is run by Liang Wenfeng's uncle...

Each test/case isn't just listed — it comes with its own commentary and emotion, like a mini comedy bit.

## 9. Sentence Fragments for Impact

Using extremely short sentences or phrases as standalone paragraphs to create pause and weight:

> Dark Forest.

> What a time to be alive, my friends.

> Security, security, and more goddamn security.

> Time.
> The passing of it.

> The teenage years of AIGC.

> No change means no information.

> No need for a subscription anymore.

> K...I...M...I...

You can't do this in every paragraph, but at emotional peaks or turning points, the effect is devastating.

## 10. Humor Techniques

**Absurd analogies:**
> Is my crawfish gold-plated? Five grand for a single installation???
> At one point I genuinely wondered if "door-to-door installation" meant someone was literally coming to install a crawfish in my house.

> Felt like I'd paid for Jensen Huang to personally walk me through the OpenClaw installation over Zoom.

> Something tells me this shop is run by Liang Wenfeng's uncle...

**Twisting famous quotes:**
> "The professors teaching your career guidance class may have never actually had a career."
> The guy installing OpenClaw for you might not even use OpenClaw himself...

**Deadpan commentary:**
> It rambled on about a bunch of nothing, then said: a circle.
> A circle? Seriously?

> Looks like it'll be better than Demon Slayer...

> Shoutout to @ShrimpBao for this masterpiece.
> Never thought I'd see the day I became an aesthetic white-shirt guy...

The core principle: humor flows naturally — it's not trying to be funny. It comes from pinpointing the absurdity in real life.

## 12. AI Draft vs Khazix Revision Comparisons

**AI wrote:** When this teammate first joined, their professional skills weren't exactly outstanding, but they had one trait — they wanted to try every new tool. They were using Claude from day one, and they were better with Midjourney than anyone else.

**Khazix revised:** When this teammate first joined, their professional skills weren't exactly outstanding, but they had one trait — they wanted to try every new tool. They were on DeepResearch from day one, Claude Code and the like way ahead of most people, and the moment something like Clawbot dropped, they were all over it.

Difference: Tool names are more specific, more current. Colloquial tone is more thorough ("and the like," "way ahead of most people").

**AI wrote:** Let me give you an example. Once, I needed to batch-process some images. In the past, I might have had to ask a colleague who knows Python for help.

**Khazix revised:** Like right now, I'm working on scraping my own content performance data from across the internet, crunching it into a Feishu multi-dimensional spreadsheet for storage, then running AI analysis directly on top of it.

Difference: Swapped a hypothetical "once, I needed to" with a real "this is what I'm actually doing right now" scenario.

**AI wrote:** We don't clock in, we don't track attendance, we don't care how many hours you work each day. Someone who knows how to use AI might work six hours a day and produce more than someone who doesn't use AI working ten hours.

**Khazix revised:** We don't clock in, we don't track attendance, we don't care how many hours you work each day. Honestly, in the AI era, I really feel like "hours worked" has become a completely meaningless metric. Someone who knows how to use AI might work two hours a day and produce more than someone who doesn't use AI working ten hours.

Difference: Added "honestly, I really feel like" as a personal judgment. Changed six to two for more extreme impact. Using digits instead of written-out numbers feels more conversational.

## 13. Intentional Disruptions in Argumentation

Deliberately inserting colloquial breaks into logical arguments, breaking the air-tightness so it reads like a real person talking:

**Repetition for emphasis:**
> No particular reason — just a whim, just a plain and simple urge to see what the current ecosystem looks like.

Three "just"s in a row, deliberately amplifying the conversational feel. Replace it with "Out of curiosity, I wanted to understand the current ecosystem" and you lose all the human warmth.

**Mid-sentence vagueness:**
> He was using some free domestic model — I'm not going to say which one.

"I'm not going to say which one" creates a coy, gossipy conversational vibe, like a little aside between friends.

**Truncation and interruption:**
> I thought about it for a sec and didn't quite figure it out.

Colloquial to the extreme — not even grammatically complete — but reads as utterly authentic.

## 14. Acknowledging the Opposing Viewpoint

Before stating your position, fully inhabit the other side's perspective so the reader feels "he gets me":

> I completely understand how that feels.
>
> You're not a programmer — you don't need to write code. You're not a content creator — you don't need to write every day. You're just a regular office worker handling mundane tasks all day. What can AI even do for you?
>
> The answer is actually simpler than you think.

First, paint the other person's situation in vivid detail. Acknowledge that their confusion is reasonable. Only then offer your own perspective. A hundred times more convincing than jumping straight to "AI is actually super useful."

## 15. Honest Learning Curves in Methodology

Good methodology doesn't just show the end goal — it honestly tells you how painful the beginning will be:

> At first, it might feel clumsy. It might take even longer than doing it manually.
>
> But after a few weeks of sticking with it, you'll notice you're saving more and more time, and your understanding of AI keeps deepening.
>
> This process is how you gradually become "AI Native."
>
> Look back a few months later, and you'll realize you've already grown into a towering tree.

Don't hide the early inefficiency, but use the "compound growth" metaphor to give readers confidence.

## 16. Absurdist Enlightenment Through Reverse Argument

Set up expectations, shatter them, then deepen the insight:

> It's like climbing all the way to the top of Mount Everest to seek out the legendary grandmaster, only for the grandmaster to stroke his beard and reveal that the ultimate martial arts secret is five words: **"Just brute-force it."**
>
> Seriously?
>
> Pure B-movie absurdity.
>
> But hold on — don't laugh just yet.

This process puts the reader's emotions on a roller coaster: anticipation → disappointment → amusement → "wait... actually, that makes sense?"

## 17. Questions as Pacing Brakes

Use questions to make the reader pause for a second, readying them for a new block of information:

> Why would copying something twice actually work?
>
> The paper gives a very engineering-heavy explanation...

> Sounds hard to follow, right?
>
> Let me explain it in plain language with an example.

The first question is a genuine follow-up; the second empathizes with the reader and promises simplification.

---

## 18. Peeling Back Layers

Don't jump straight to the conclusion. Unfold progressively: phenomenon → surface explanation → deeper questioning → core insight:

> We often say AI has gone mainstream, but really, we're still operating within a tiny bubble.
>
> We haven't reached the late stage of AI — we haven't even reached the beginning of the middle stage. We're just getting started.
>
> 84% of the world's population has never had a single AI conversation.
>
> Think about what the internet looked like when only 16% of people were online — that was roughly around 2005...

Each sentence builds on the previous one, pushing one layer deeper. The reader thinks alongside you, rather than passively receiving conclusions.

---

## 19. The Advanced Art of Humble Prefacing

In a methodology article, don't just say "I'm not sure." Genuinely express the psychological weight of writing this kind of piece:

> Honestly, writing advice-type articles always weighs on me.
>
> Because I'm no success story myself. I'm still figuring things out, still making mistakes. Who am I to give anyone advice?
>
> But after this trip home, something shifted in my thinking.

This stance of "I'm honestly conflicted about whether I should even be writing this" builds more trust than jumping straight into the methodology.
